# Research Methodology

How to conduct expert-level research when building a skill.

## Goal

Your target is expert-tier knowledge: nuances, edge cases, and counterintuitive insights that separate remarkable output from merely good output.

## Research Protocol

### 1. Inventory Existing Knowledge

Before searching, state your hypotheses and confidence levels about expert practice in this domain. This reveals which claims need verification and where your actual knowledge gaps are -- focus research there.

### 2. Research Broadly and Deeply

Search from multiple angles -- these can be interleaved, not sequential:

**Map the landscape**: Who are the authorities? What vocabulary do practitioners use? What frameworks or methodologies dominate?

**Study expert methodology**: Search for HOW experts work, not just WHAT they produce. Seek primary artifacts -- actual code, documents, designs, or guidelines by experts. These contain more embedded knowledge than articles *about* the domain.
- "[domain] professional workflow"
- "[domain] advanced techniques"

**Find tradeoffs and disagreements**: Expert knowledge lives where authorities disagree. Document both perspectives and the conditions where each applies.
- "[domain] controversy" or "[domain] debate"
- "[domain] overrated practices" or "[domain] myths"

**Study anti-patterns**: What beginners get wrong, what intermediates miss, what separates good from great.
- "[domain] common mistakes professionals avoid"

### 3. Check Recency

Verify your findings reflect current practice. Search for recent standards and shifts -- but recency alone is not authority. A ratified standard outranks a recent blog post regardless of date.

### Stopping Criterion

Stop researching when you can:
1. Articulate the difference between intermediate and expert-level work in this domain
2. Name at least 3 specific, actionable principles that a non-expert would not know
3. Describe the hardest judgment call in the domain and provide a decision framework for it

If you cannot do all three, keep researching. If searches are producing diminishing returns or web sources are exhausted, acknowledge the gap explicitly and move forward with what you have.

**Negative stopping criteria** -- stop and escalate if:
- The domain is too broad to encode in a single skill (propose splitting)
- Research keeps producing contradictory advice without resolution (document the disagreement as a decision framework instead)
- The skill concept is fundamentally flawed (tell the user and propose an alternative)

## Source Quality

Prioritize in this order:
1. **Official documentation** -- authoritative source of truth
2. **Expert practitioners** -- published books, conference talks, demonstrated track record
3. **Peer-reviewed / editorial** -- curated publications, edited guides
4. **Quality community** -- well-regarded blogs, canonical Stack Overflow answers
5. **General** -- verify against higher-quality sources before trusting

**Red flags**: Generic listicles, AI-generated slop, outdated content, no author attribution, contradicts multiple authoritative sources.

**For domains without official docs** (soft skills, creative fields): practitioner content becomes the top tier. Look for books, talks, and detailed case studies from recognized experts.

## Synthesis

Raw research is not useful until synthesized into actionable principles.

### Principle Extraction
- What do ALL experts agree on? (fundamental principles)
- Where do experts DISAGREE? (judgment calls -- document conditions for each approach)
- What do only the BEST experts mention? (expert-tier insights)

### Decision Frameworks
Map the key decisions a practitioner makes. For each: what factors matter, what the options are, how to choose. This turns vague expertise into concrete logic.

### Quality Spectrum
Define what bad, acceptable, excellent, and exceptional look like. Use these as calibration points in the skill -- but compress them for the final output. The spectrum is a research tool, not something to reproduce wholesale.

**When sources contradict**: Prefer the option with stronger empirical evidence. If evidence is equal, make it a decision framework with explicit criteria for when each approach applies.

**After synthesis, force-rank your findings**: Which 5 principles have the highest impact on output quality? Lead with those in the skill.
